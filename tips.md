# Keynote Tips

## Opening the conference

- **Acknowledge the room, not yourself.** People traveled to be here. Thank them
  for showing up. "You being here is what makes this real" hits harder than talking
  about yourself.
- **Be a host, not just a speaker.** For the first 30-60 seconds, you're welcoming
  people into your house. Warm, relaxed energy. If you're nervous, that's fine —
  saying "I'm genuinely thrilled to be here" is honest and lands well.
- **Set expectations for the conference.** What kind of event is this? What should
  people do between talks? If you want people talking to each other, say it
  explicitly: "go introduce yourself to someone you don't know."
- **Don't rush into content.** Give the room a moment to settle. Let the welcome
  section breathe.

## Content considerations

- **Thank the organizers and sponsors.** Quick and sincere — don't read a list.
- **Logistics reminder?** Check with organizers if they want you to cover lunch,
  after-party, etc. or if someone else handles it.
- **The audience might be nervous too.** First conference for the community. Some
  people might not know anyone. Give them permission to be social ("grab someone
  at lunch and ask what they're building").
- **You're speaking as the project leader, not a company.** Tokio is community-driven,
  not corporate-owned. Lean into that — does it deserve a mention?
- **What do you want people to *do* after your talk?** Not just "feel inspired" —
  is there a concrete action? Think about Rust at the app level? Try Toasty? Talk
  to Alice? Build a library?

## Delivery

- **Pacing:** 25 minutes feels short when writing, long on stage. Practice out
  loud at least twice. Nerves speed you up — better to end at 22 minutes with
  room to breathe than rush through 30 minutes of material.
- **Water.** Bring water on stage. You will need it.
- **The bold prediction is the riskiest moment.** You'll feel the room react.
  Don't backpedal in the moment. Either commit fully or soften it in the outline
  now. Qualifying it on stage ("well, maybe not *most*...") will undermine
  everything you just built.
